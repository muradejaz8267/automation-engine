Question 1
A 64-year-old man presents to the emergency department with 45 minutes of crushing substernal chest pain radiating to his left arm. He has a history of hypertension, type 2 diabetes mellitus, and hyperlipidemia. Blood pressure is 88/60 mm Hg, heart rate is 112/min, and respiratory rate is 24/min. Physical examination reveals cool extremities and jugular venous distension. ECG shows ST-segment elevations in leads II, III, and aVF. Initial troponin I is elevated. He is given aspirin and heparin. Shortly afterward, he becomes acutely hypotensive with worsening dyspnea and pulmonary crackles.
Which of the following is the most appropriate next step in management?
Answer Choices:
(A) Intravenous nitroglycerin
(B) Dobutamine infusion
(C) Immediate percutaneous coronary intervention
(D) Intravenous furosemide
(E) Intra-aortic balloon pump placement
Correct Answer: E
Explanation:
This patient has an inferior STEMI complicated by cardiogenic shock, suggested by hypotension, JVD, pulmonary edema, and cool extremities. Inferior MIs can involve the right ventricle, making preload dependence critical. An intra-aortic balloon pump improves coronary perfusion and decreases afterload as a temporizing measure in cardiogenic shock prior to definitive reperfusion.
* (A) Nitroglycerin reduces preload and would worsen hypotension.
* (B) Dobutamine may help later but does not address mechanical support needs.
* (C) PCI is definitive but immediate hemodynamic stabilization is required first.
* (D) Furosemide worsens preload-dependent shock.
