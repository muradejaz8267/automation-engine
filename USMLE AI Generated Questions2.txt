Question 1
A 62-year-old man with long-standing type 2 diabetes mellitus presents with progressive dyspnea and lower-extremity edema. Blood pressure is 158/96 mm Hg. Laboratory studies show serum creatinine of 2.4 mg/dL. Urinalysis demonstrates proteinuria without hematuria. Renal biopsy shows nodular mesangial expansion with periodic acid–Schiff–positive acellular nodules and thickened glomerular basement membranes. Which mechanism most directly contributes to the development of these nodular lesions?
Answer Choices:
 (A) Immune complex deposition with complement activation
(B) Increased nonenzymatic glycosylation of matrix proteins
(C) Podocyte foot process effacement due to T-cell cytokines
(D) Endothelial injury from anti–glomerular basement membrane antibodies
(E) Reduced nephrin expression from congenital mutation
Correct Answer: B
Explanation:
 The biopsy findings describe Kimmelstiel–Wilson nodules of diabetic nephropathy. Chronic hyperglycemia leads to nonenzymatic glycosylation of basement membrane and mesangial matrix proteins, increasing matrix production and cross-linking, which causes nodular mesangial sclerosis.
(A) Immune complex deposition characterizes diseases such as membranous nephropathy or lupus nephritis.
(C) Podocyte cytokine-mediated injury is typical of minimal change disease.
(D) Anti-GBM antibodies cause linear IgG deposition, not nodular sclerosis.
(E) Nephrin mutations cause congenital nephrotic syndrome, not diabetic nodular lesions.
