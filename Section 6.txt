Question 21
A 9-year-old boy is brought to the clinic for evaluation of developmental delay and episodic ataxia. His parents report that symptoms worsen during periods of fasting or illness. Physical examination reveals hypotonia and mild hepatomegaly. Laboratory studies show elevated serum lactate, alanine, and pyruvate levels, along with hypoglycemia. Muscle biopsy demonstrates normal glycogen structure but reduced oxidative phosphorylation capacity.
Which of the following enzymatic deficiencies most directly explains this patient’s metabolic derangements?
A. Pyruvate carboxylase
B. Pyruvate dehydrogenase complex
C. Fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase
D. Glycogen phosphorylase
E. Carnitine acyltransferase I
Correct Answer: B
Explanation:
 Pyruvate dehydrogenase complex (PDH) deficiency prevents conversion of pyruvate to acetyl-CoA, shunting pyruvate toward lactate and alanine production. Pyruvate carboxylase deficiency also causes lactic acidosis but would impair gluconeogenesis more directly and show different biochemical patterns.

Question 22
A researcher studies an enzyme involved in nucleotide synthesis. Increasing substrate concentration increases reaction velocity until a plateau is reached. When a competitive inhibitor is added, the maximal velocity remains unchanged, but a higher substrate concentration is required to reach half-maximal velocity.
Which of the following best describes the effect of the inhibitor on enzyme kinetics?
A. Decreased Vmax with unchanged Km
B. Increased Vmax with increased Km
C. Unchanged Vmax with increased Km
D. Decreased Vmax with decreased Km
E. Unchanged Vmax with unchanged Km

Explanation:
 Competitive inhibitors compete with substrate at the active site. This increases Km (more substrate needed to reach ½ Vmax) while Vmax remains unchanged, as sufficiently high substrate concentrations can outcompete the inhibitor.

B. Catalase converting hydrogen peroxide to water and oxygen
C. Glutathione peroxidase reducing hydrogen peroxide using GSH
