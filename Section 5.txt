Question 1
A 62-year-old man with chronic atrial fibrillation is started on warfarin. Five days later, he develops painful, necrotic skin lesions on his abdomen. Laboratory studies show prolonged PT and normal platelet count. Which mechanism best explains this adverse effect?
Answer Choices:
 (A) Direct inhibition of platelet aggregation
(B) Autoimmune antibody–mediated destruction of platelets
(C) Rapid decline in protein C leading to a hypercoagulable state
(D) Accumulation of vitamin K in the liver
(E) Increased fibrinolysis due to plasmin activation
Correct Answer: C
Explanation:
 Warfarin inhibits vitamin K–dependent clotting factors II, VII, IX, X and anticoagulants protein C and S. Protein C has a short half-life, so its rapid decline can transiently create a hypercoagulable state, causing skin necrosis.
(A) Platelet aggregation inhibition is by antiplatelet drugs, not warfarin.
(B) Heparin-induced thrombocytopenia is immune-mediated with low platelets.
(D) Vitamin K accumulation would antagonize warfarin.
(E) Plasmin activation does not occur with warfarin.
